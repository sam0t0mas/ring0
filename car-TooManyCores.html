There was a time when a laptop was something you brought into a meeting, opened quietly, and used primarily to pretend you were paying attention.

Then Intel engineers decided that what laptops really needed was enough computing power to launch a medium-sized nation into orbit.

Enter the Core Ultra 9 285HX. A processor so powerful it makes last year's workstations look like public library computers.

A Completely Senseless Number of Cores

The spec sheet reads like the sort of thing normally found attached to a rack server hidden deep inside a datacentre. Twenty-four cores. Eight Performance cores. Sixteen Efficiency cores. Massive cache. 5.5GHz boost clocks.

Twenty years ago you'd need several CPUs and a dedicated server room to achieve this level of grunt. Now it's available next to your RGB keyboard.

"Buying a 285HX-powered laptop for Microsoft Word is like purchasing an aircraft carrier to visit Aldi."

Gaming Has Officially Become Ridiculous

Pair this thing with an RTX-class GPU and you've essentially created a portable supercomputer disguised as a gaming laptop.

Games? Easy. Streaming? Easy. Video encoding? Easy. Discord, Spotify, Chrome and six monitoring tools? The processor barely notices.

For Infrastructure Nerds, This Is Basically Pornography

Virtual machines. Containers. Kubernetes clusters. Lab environments. Entire fake datacentres living inside VMware Workstation.

The Core Ultra 9 285HX exists for the sort of people who hear "24-core CPU" and immediately open PowerCLI.

Your average person sees a laptop. A consultant sees a mobile cloud region.

The Problem of Physics

Officially this is a 55W processor. Unofficially every laptop manufacturer has interpreted that figure as merely an opening suggestion.

Under heavy load these systems become extraordinarily warm. Not "that's a bit toasty" warm. More "small apartment heating solution" warm.

The Stig's Verdict

Some say its cooling system can alter local weather patterns. Some say VMware administrators can hear its fans from orbit. All we know is this: Intel has crammed desktop-class performance into a laptop, and the result is utterly glorious.

The Core Ultra 9 285HX isn't sensible. It isn't restrained. It definitely isn't subtle. But if your computing needs involve gaming, content creation, development, or running enough virtual machines to simulate a Fortune 500 company, this is currently about as good as mobile silicon gets.
