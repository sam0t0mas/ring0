THE MACHINES THAT MADE MY CAREER

IBM XT → IBM PS/1 → clones → laptops → labs → Linux → networks → modern homelab

My entire career — every skill, every system I’ve built, every network I’ve architected — traces back to the machines I grew up with. I didn’t learn technology from textbooks. I learned it from hardware that crashed, software that misbehaved, and systems that demanded experimentation. This is my story, told through the computers that shaped me.

IBM XT (1989)

The IBM XT was my gateway into computing. A machine that looked more like industrial equipment than a personal device, humming quietly as it booted into DOS. I spent countless hours exploring its directory structure, learning how files were organised, and discovering how fragile early software could be.

  • ~4 MHz CPU
  • 1 MB RAM
  • Monochrome monitor
  • DOS, BASIC, Thexder

IBM PS/1 (1991)

The PS/1 felt futuristic compared to the XT. It was faster, smoother, and came with IBM’s attempt at a more user-friendly DOS environment. But I ignored the friendly interface and dove straight into the raw system underneath.
