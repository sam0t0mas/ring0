THE MACHINES THAT MADE MY CAREER

IBM XT → IBM PS/1 → clones, laptops, labs, Linux, networks, and decades of curiosity

My career in technology is really the story of the machines I grew up with. Every system taught me something new — sometimes by working perfectly, more often by breaking in spectacular ways. This is my personal timeline, told through the hardware that shaped me.

IBM XT (1989)

My first computer. Beige. Heavy. Glorious.

  • ~4 MHz CPU
  • 1 MB RAM
  • DOS
  • Thexder

I learned DOS by editing CONFIG.SYS and AUTOEXEC.BAT until something broke, then figuring out how to fix it. That loop became the foundation of everything I do.

IBM PS/1 (1991)

  • 10 MHz CPU
  • 2 MB RAM
  • IBM PC DOS
  • Commander Keen

My first “fast” machine. I pushed it hard, learned memory managers, and discovered how DOS games really worked under the hood.

The Clone Era (1993–1998)

386 Clone (1993)

  • MS-DOS
  • DOOM
  • IRQ conflicts everywhere

486SX Clone (1995)

  • MS-DOS, Windows 3.11, Windows 95
  • Novell IPX/SPX networking

These machines taught me hardware the hard way — jumpers, BIOS quirks, sound card drama, and the joy of squeezing performance out of something I built myself.

Early Linux & Networking: Wallace and Gromit

286 “Wallace”

  • NIS, SMB
  • Postfix, Sendmail
  • Production Red Hat Linux

286 “Gromit”

  • BGP
  • iptables
  • INET gateway duties

These were my first real production Linux systems. They handled authentication, mail routing, firewalling, and gateway services. Running real infrastructure taught me real responsibility.

Final Reflection

From a 4 MHz IBM XT to a 24‑core Minisforum powerhouse, every machine has been a chapter in my story. Decades later, I’m still learning, still building, still exploring — because the curiosity that started in 1989 never went away.
