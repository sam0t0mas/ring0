USED CAR FIGHT CLUB

Top Gear-ish Online Magazine • June 2004 • Dial-up Friendly*

1982 Holden Camira JB vs 1996 Mitsubishi TJ Magna

One was Australia's future. The other survived long enough to become Australia's past.

Remember the early 1980s? People thought we'd have moon bases, robot servants and flying cars by the year 2000. Instead we got reality TV, Facebook and the Holden Camira.

"Some people say the Camira was ahead of its time. Unfortunately, its reliability was behind several centuries."
— Jeremy Clarkson (probably)

The Camira was General Motors' grand vision for Australian motoring. It was front-wheel drive, modern, advanced and packed with innovative engineering. Which is another way of saying it was completely unprepared for being owned by actual human beings.

ROUND ONE: LOOKS

1982 Holden Camira JB

From a distance the Camira doesn't actually look that bad. Back in 1982 it appeared sleek, European and sophisticated. Today it resembles a photocopy of a Vauxhall Cavalier left out in the rain for 40 years.

"It's got all the visual excitement of an accountant's briefcase."
— Clarkson

Every angle suggests speed. Every mechanical component suggests otherwise.

1996 Mitsubishi TJ Magna

The Magna is so anonymous that if you parked one in your driveway, you'd come home later and ask who left a Magna in your driveway.

It wasn't styled. It was merely approved.

"It's not ugly. It's just committed itself fully to being utterly forgettable."
— Clarkson

ROUND TWO: PERFORMANCE

Camira

The Camira's four-cylinder engine generates a modest amount of forward momentum. Press the accelerator and the car begins considering your request.

Five seconds later it forms a committee. Three seconds after that it schedules an action plan. Then eventually, with enough encouragement, it may accelerate.

"0–100 km/h takes so long that by the time you've arrived, your warranty has expired."
— Clarkson

Overtaking requires courage, determination and occasionally a tailwind.

Magna

The Magna V6 changed everything. Suddenly Australian families could join motorway traffic without first updating their wills.

The V6 is smooth, eager and surprisingly brisk. Not sports-car brisk. More 'large Labrador chasing a tennis ball' brisk.

"Compared with the Camira, this feels less like a car and more like witchcraft."
— Clarkson

ROUND THREE: BUILD QUALITY

Camira

This is where things become genuinely fascinating.

Camira reliability stories are now part of Australian folklore. Owners would wake up every morning wondering what new and exciting fault the car had developed overnight.

  • Water leaks
  • Electrical gremlins
  • Trim falling off
  • Random rattles
  • Existential despair
"The Camira wasn't assembled so much as loosely supervised."
— Clarkson

Legend says some Camiras developed faults while still being unloaded from the transporter.

Magna

Meanwhile the Magna operates on a completely different principle:

Actually finishing the job.

These things refuse to die. Ignore servicing. Forget maintenance. Use cheap petrol. Miss oil changes. The Magna simply shrugs and keeps driving.

"If nuclear winter comes, you'll find three surviving species: cockroaches, Nokia 3310s and V6 Magnas."
— Clarkson

ROUND FOUR: INTERIOR

Camira

The dashboard appears to have been moulded from recycled Tupperware. The seats are less supportive than an unsympathetic school counsellor.

Everything creaks. Everything flexes. Everything feels temporary.

"I've sat on more comfortable garden furniture."
— Clarkson

Magna

The Magna interior is vast. The rear seats have enough room for three adults, four children and a medium-sized suburban grievance.

The boot appears capable of swallowing:

  • Camping equipment
  • Two bicycles
  • Half a Bunnings warehouse
  • The Camira's resale value

VERDICT

The Holden Camira deserves respect. Not because it was good. Because it taught Australia an important lesson:

Never confuse technology with competence.

The Magna, meanwhile, is one of the unsung heroes of Australian motoring. Nobody dreamed about owning one. Nobody put posters of one on their bedroom wall.

But unlike the Camira, it usually made it home.

"The Camira was Australia's attempt at building the future. The Magna was Australia's apology letter."
— Clarkson

Post Script: Why Write This?

Fair question.

After all, this is nearly 3,000 words dedicated to two cars that, according to most of the internet, should have been traded in for a bus ticket sometime around the Howard Government.

But cars are funny things.

To some people they're transport. To others, status symbols. To a very specific type of tragic enthusiast, they're a collection of spreadsheets connected by bolts.

To me, they've always represented something else entirely:

Freedom.

Not freedom in the Corvette commercial sense where you're carving up a mountain road while inspirational music plays.

Actual freedom.

The ability to leave. The ability to explore. The ability to discover that the town two hours away is exactly the same as your town, except with a different bakery.

A car creates what sociologists call a third space. Not home. Not work. Just yours.

A rolling little bubble where the world's problems exist somewhere beyond the windscreen.

"Cars are not about transportation. They're about possibility."
— Clarkson-ish

Which brings us to the important bit.

I've owned both of these cars.

The JB Camira cost me exactly $100. One hundred Australian dollars. Less than a modern headlight globe. Less than a tank of fuel in 2026. Less than some people spend on keyboard switches.

And it came with free driving lessons. Thanks Fez. You're either an excellent teacher or a reckless optimist.

The Camira was held together by rust, determination and what I'm reasonably certain was original 1982 dirt.

Every bolt looked as though it'd survived several maritime incidents.

It was terrible.

And it was magnificent.

Because for $100 the world suddenly got bigger.

The Camira wasn't a car. It was a key.

A rattly, leaking, questionably-engineered key.

But a key nonetheless.

Then came the TJ Magna.

Purchased in 2011 for the princely sum of $3,500.

Again, a collection of ageing Australian metallurgy held together mostly by habit.

But now there was a V6.

And for the first time I understood why engine swaps exist.

The Magna delivered exactly the same feeling as the Camira.

The freedom. The independence. The ability to point at a map and simply go there.

Only now with enough power to overtake something larger than a ride-on lawnmower.

"The Camira introduced me to motoring. The Magna introduced me to acceleration."

Both cars were objectively worth very little.

Both cars were rusting towards retirement.

Both cars probably consumed more oil than they should have.

Yet both delivered value far beyond their purchase price.

Which creates an interesting problem.

Because recently I spent $5,300 of my own money building a VCF lab.

That's before storage.

Before switching.

Before software.

Before all the little purchases that somehow appear every time you tell yourself:

"This is the last thing I need to buy."

Any homelab enthusiast reading this already knows that's a lie.

A terrible lie.

The sort of lie you tell yourself moments before ordering another rack server at 2 a.m.

Unlike the Camira or the Magna, this collection of enterprise hardware will almost certainly never hold its value.

Nobody will buy it in twenty years because they're nostalgic for fourth-generation Xeons.

Nobody will stop at a car show and say:

"Mate, remember the legendary Intel X710 network adapter?"

There will be no VCF owner's clubs. No Sunday drives. No magazine features.

Only electricity bills.

And firmware updates.

Lots and lots of firmware updates.

"Cars lose value. Servers evaporate value."

But then I remember something.

The Camira wasn't valuable because it was a Camira.

The Magna wasn't valuable because it was a Magna.

They were valuable because they opened doors.

They created possibilities.

They made the world larger.

And in a strange way this pile of servers does exactly the same thing.

The only consolation I have is that RAM is cheaper than ever.

Unfortunately, self-control remains stubbornly expensive.
